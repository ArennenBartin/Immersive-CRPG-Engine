# THE THIRD VOICE
### An Alderamontico Trial — Adventure Treatment, v2

**What changed from v1:** the three points of interest are no longer parallel exhibits of three philosophies. Each is now a self-contained mini-trial with named people and a crisis already in motion, and all three are driven by the *same active source* — the girl at the stone — who is not cargo to be argued over but a half-made anchor drowning three communities just by being what she is. The three crises are three faces of one surrender, and they converge because all three factions independently decide the answer is at the Stone, with her, in the form of a rite that ends someone. The plot now runs *through* her instead of around her.

---

## 1. Frame

One game, one trial. The first of several that make the long road to the Spire, where — games from now — a person becomes, or refuses to become, the Anchor. This trial's job is the first one: take an ordinary stranger and turn them into a *candidate who knows* — who learns, by the end, that there is a road, that there is a thing called the Anchor at its end, and that the voice they have been hearing is not God.

Ethereal philosophical fantasy. Wonder and grief, not scares; the dread is conceptual and lives in what the player understands, carried by graphics and moment-to-moment writing. An adventure, not an investigation: you arrive into crises in motion and *act*, you don't reconstruct a cold case. A region, hub-and-spoke, three points of interest in any order, converging at a central square.

---

## 2. The Grid, as it works here

Stated once. The Grid does not reshape places; it reshapes their *feeling.* It draws off whatever emotion lives strongest in a place and pours it back through Glass, amplified, so the place drowns in an intensified version of itself, and it feeds on the excess. Glass is its residue and its organ; where it has worked, Glass grows.

Underneath: the cosmos is silent. Nothing transcendent answers. The Dark Lights are sacred language, not a real afterlife. So the only thing that *ever* answers a grief or a longing is the Grid, reflecting your own reaching back at you until it feels like being heard. Every felt presence of the divine in Alderamontico is your own energy sold back to you by a parasite. The Church worships the Grid without knowing it, because the Grid, fed on their devotion, gives them the answered prayer their faith promised.

An **Anchor** is a single coherent person who gives the diffuse Grid a *self* — a center, a will, a face. The trials cultivate someone whole enough to be worth wearing. To become the Anchor is to have mastered all the philosophies and integrated them into one statement; that is exactly why the Grid wants the most integrated person it can grow. The protagonist never hears the word *Grid.* They hear a voice.

---

## 3. The protagonist and the want

A stranger **Intercessor** — one who stands between worlds and carries words without becoming their mouth. No side's native. Their whole discipline is non-belonging.

**The want, which the trial turns on:** to finally *belong somewhere.* To be on a side, to be home, to stop being the one who carries words and never keeps them. This is the bait — because the voice does not only promise to end the dispute; it promises *them a place.* It agrees with them as no side ever has. It makes them feel chosen. To become the reconciling center everyone meets at — the Anchor — *is* to finally belong, and refusing it means choosing to remain the one who belongs nowhere, forever. That is the climax's true cost.

**Why this person hears the voice when almost no one does:** the strongest living thing in them is that ache to be answered, and the Grid feeds it back like a tuning fork. Most people's longing is diffuse; theirs is one clean lifelong note.

**The assignment (a thread, never resolved this game):** a sealed commission with a seal the Intercessor doesn't recognize. They believe they were *sent to mediate.* They were *placed.* The commission traces, distantly, to the corrupt head of the Church — the one person alive who knows the Anchor exists and seeds candidates into trials. The Threefold March, with its stranded girl, is a ripening-ground. None of this is learned this game. It is the door to the next.

---

## 4. The girl at the stone — the active source

A young person found at the Stone, Glass-marked, silent, maybe dying, maybe something else. She is not the object of the dispute. She is its *cause,* and she is causing it right now.

She is a **half-made anchor** — a prior candidate who walked a trial before you and got *further,* now caught at the threshold: not Completed, not free. She is the mirror: what you become if you say yes. And she is a *concentration point.* The Grid reaches through her like a lens. Whatever emotion lives strongest near her, she amplifies and pours back, drowning it. She is not doing this on purpose; she may not be willing it at all. She is a wound the Grid speaks through. This is why each faction-seat is in crisis — each is drowning in its own defining emotion, amplified through her, and the closer you draw to her, the clearer the source becomes.

**She answers a little, and how she answers is the engine of the ending.** Early and middle, her answers come in the voice's service — she is mostly lens, barely herself, and what surfaces flatters your want, draws you toward the Stone, toward yes. You cannot tell her from the voice, because she half-isn't herself. The cracks are faint and only a player attending closely catches them: a word that's hers and not the voice's, a flinch under the comfort. **What changes her is the player's own practice.** Long Attention, in this world, calls the particular up out of the borrowed story — and the more faithfully you attend *her* rather than the voice through her, the more of the real person you raise through the lens. At the climax, for one bought moment, your attention wins over the amplification and she surfaces — and with the only selfhood she has left she spends it not on her own rescue but on *yours.* (See §10.) Then the lens closes back over her, because attention can surface the particular but cannot free it. Freeing her is not this trial's road.

---

## 5. The region and its shape

**The Threefold March** — a borderland valley where three traditions share one ground and one century-old quarrel: the **Watchfold**, an old-world country of grave-roads and threshold-fields and the watchers who tend them; **the Combe**, a Church parish in a hollow; and **the Marrowhouse**, a seat of the Crimson Ledger. At the center is **the Convening** — a square around a single **Stone** that is grave and shrine and covenant-mark at once, claimed by all three, owned by none. The girl is there.

Three points of interest — the three faction-seats — explorable in any order. The Convening is visitable from the first hour, quiet, where the Stone and the girl sit.

**The convergence is dramatic, not administrative.** You do not tie three abstract threads. Each POI is a faction *coming apart,* and resolving it (any way) ends with that faction *processing to the Stone* to perform its rite. All three independently conclude the answer is at the Convening, with the girl — and all three answers take the shape of a rite that ends someone:

- the Watchfold comes to **sit the long vigil** over her, unto death;
- the Combe comes to **Complete** its whole grieving parish through her, in one rite;
- the Marrowhouse comes to **release** by consent — the fade.

Paralysis-unto-death, Completion, and the fade: three faces of the one surrender the Grid wants, three sincere mercies, each summoned by the voice, each centered on her. You are the only person who carries all three and has seen they are the same act.

---

## 6. POINT OF INTEREST — THE WATCHFOLD
### *The long vigil*

**The drowning:** the old world's defining note is reverent attention. Through the girl it has become paralysis. The Watchfold is a country sitting down and not getting up.

**The rite, and how it ends someone:** the watchers have an honored practice — when a thing will not answer, the most faithful witness *takes up the long vigil,* sitting with the unanswerable until it speaks, even unto death. This is the highest fidelity their tradition knows. Through the girl it has become a slow contagion: vigil after vigil sat at the Stone and never rising, each death read by the others as devotion rather than as the girl pulling them under. The Watchfold is not in crisis the way a fire is; it is in crisis the way a held breath is, and the breath is being held to death.

**The driver — Wenna.** The grave-listener who found the girl and sat the first vigil. She is right, in the old way: to name the girl too soon is to end what she actually is. But she has attended so totally and so long that she cannot rise, because to rise is to admit the answer is not coming, and her whole life forbids that admission. She is dying at the Stone's edge and calls it faith.

**The inside dissenter, losing — Ode.** Wenna's student. Ode can see that the watch has become a grave and has broken the oldest taboo by trying to *raise* the sitters before they die — and is shunned for impiety, for "interrupting the answer," for cowardice dressed as care. Ode is right and alone, and being unmade by the people they're trying to save.

**The willing victim, the knife — Maren.** An old watcher whose spouse died this season, who is about to take up the long vigil to honor that death — gladly, devoutly, as the most loving thing a person can do. You can stop Maren only by violating the thing Maren holds most sacred: the right to sit faithfully with one's own grief unto the end. Saving Maren means telling a grieving person their devotion is a death the girl is feeding on — and being, to them, exactly the impious interloper the Watchfold already fears.

**What you do (adventure, exploration-first):** the Watchfold is the March's most open and strangest terrain — grave-roads, threshold-fields, weather-lines, the long country between graves. You walk vigils. You do the slow work that lets the paralyzed act without overruling them — siding with Ode, or finding the thing that lets Wenna rise without betraying her fidelity, or reaching Maren before the sitting. Combat comes from the night roads, where the region's wildlife and its wraiths are grief amplified into teeth — the same drowning, made hostile.

**The shallow path vs the true path (this feeds the Stone — see §9):** you can break the paralysis the shallow way — give the Watchfold the answer it's waiting for, declare the girl, let them rise relieved — which calms the symptom and feeds her, because a declared answer is a closure the Grid pulls through. Or the true way: teach them, by doing it, the old correction — *provisional* closure, rising without pretending the matter is closed, attending without sitting unto death. Costlier, slower, and it does not feed the lens.

**Practice granted — Long Attention.** There is no old-world companion: the old world's ethic is staying-with-a-place; it cannot send a wanderer without betraying itself. So the old world is the tradition you *internalize.* Long Attention is grown by you — mostly at the Stone, with the girl — and it is the practice that lets you see the amplification loop at every other POI, surface the girl's real self, and, eventually, turn faithful attention on the *voice* until the borrowed story cracks. The absence of an old-world companion is the design, not a gap.

**Thread to the Stone:** the long vigils are sat *at the Stone,* because that is where the unanswerable thing is. Resolving the Watchfold sends its watchers to the Convening — a slow procession of people coming to sit, whether to rise from it or not.

## 7. POINT OF INTEREST — THE COMBE
### *The mass Completion*

**The drowning:** the parish's defining note is compassion. Through the girl it has become control. The Combe comforts everyone into stillness; its mercy has stopped asking permission.

**The rite, and how it ends someone:** Completion works through Glass — a comfort-shard takes a person's unbearable grief and, instead of easing it, relocates it into self-erasure; the person goes still, content, hollow. One shard, one person, slow intimate work — the parish has never done more than one at a time. The girl has changed that. As a lens she can bind the *whole* grieving congregation's grief into a single resonance, so that one rite, poured through her at the Stone, Completes them all at once. Cael has not decided to do something monstrous; she believes she has been *given the means to finally heal everyone,* and the giver, which she hears as grace, is the voice.

**The driver — Prioress Cael.** Sincere, intelligent, genuinely kind. Constructive Consolation gone septic in the exact three-fold way the canon names: she has forgotten her comfort is *made* and calls it cosmic fact; she installs it below consent; and she has confused ending suffering with ending the sufferer. She is not a villain. She is a good shepherd who has been handed, she thinks, a miracle.

**The inside dissenter, losing — Sister Linnet.** Practices the true rite — names the comfort as made, installs nothing, consoles without erasing. She is trying to stop the mass Completion and she is losing, and being called cold and faithless for it. She is who **Brother Aldric** admires; watching Cael overrule Linnet is what fractures or deepens Aldric (see §8). Linnet is the proof, inside the Church, that the corrected faith still exists and is being smothered by the septic one.

**The willing victim, the knife — Doran.** He lost his wife, Alee. He *wants* the rite; he begs for it; Cael holds him up as living proof the Completion is mercy. To save Doran is to override a freely grieving man's plea for peace — to look at someone in real pain who has asked, lucidly, to stop hurting, and refuse him. The knife here cuts the opposite way from the Watchfold's: there you must override a devotion, here a plea.

**What you do (social, stabilization):** you arrive the night before the rite. You move through a parish drowning in its own kindness, where amplified compassion is quietly hollowing people; you stabilize where you can, side with Linnet or fail her, reach Doran, and decide what to do about a rite that is mercy to the people performing it and an erasure to the people receiving it. You can stand against it, honor it, or find the third thing. Combat comes if the parish's zeal turns militant — a Completion-rite weaponized, a saint-shell, comfort made an enforcer.

**The shallow path vs the true path (feeds the Stone):** the shallow way is to let the rite happen, or to give the parish a smaller Completion to satisfy it — which ends grief and feeds the lens, because a Completion is exactly the surrender the Grid eats. The true way is to bring the parish the corrected rite — Linnet's rite — console honestly, name the comfort as made, install nothing, and let those who still want numbness choose it (consent cuts both ways). Harder, and it leaves grief in the world, and it does not feed her.

**Practice granted — Honest Consolation.** To console while naming the comfort as made; to offer relief without installing it. This is what later lets you stabilize people spiraling at the other POIs, and what lets you comfort the girl at the climax without erasing her.

**Thread to the Stone:** the mass rite is set *at the Stone,* because only there, through her, can the whole parish be bound at once. Engaging the Combe is partly what *summons* the convergence — the parish processes to the Convening to perform the largest mercy it has ever attempted.

## 8. POINT OF INTEREST — THE MARROWHOUSE
### *The release rite*

**The drowning:** the house's defining note is scruple — consent honored absolutely. Through the girl it has become a cage. The Marrowhouse is so afraid to cross a boundary that it cannot move to save anyone; its courtesy has hardened into paralysis with perfect manners.

**The rite, and how it ends someone:** the Ledger has an honored ceremony — the *release rite,* for a guest who has chosen to fade: the ascetic fast unto ending, consent to one's own disappearance, which the corrected canon names as the Ledger's hidden nihilism, the will to nothingness wearing the law's own face. Through the girl, the house's scruple has amplified into a conviction that the *most* honorable thing — for a dying guest, for the house, perhaps for the girl herself — is the clean consented fade: take nothing, cross nothing, let go with perfect lawful dignity. Ister has begun to believe the girl should be *offered the rite* — that her covenant-mark on the Stone was already a kind of yes.

**The driver — Ister.** A vow-keeper of the old discipline. He believes, sincerely and through the voice, that the release rite is the one act in the whole March that honors consent absolutely: nobody is crossed, nobody is taken, everyone fades by their own free assent. He is right that consent is sacred and wrong that a yes to one's own erasure is the law's fulfillment rather than its hidden death-wish.

**The inside dissenter, losing — Reni.** A young vow-keeper, and the house's blind spot made flesh: Reni is *enthralled* — consent corrupted by dependency and charisma — and cannot tell, and is being lined up to give the next "free" yes. The house cannot see it, because its law checks only that a yes was *given,* never whether it was *free.* Reni is the living refutation of the house's certainty, standing in plain sight, about to consent to something Reni cannot actually choose.

**The willing victim, the knife — Orla.** An old guest who has lived long and lucidly chooses the fade. She is not deluded; she has decided, clearly and freely, to let go. To stop the release is to override a *true* refusal of life — the inverse of Doran. Doran wants the rite and the world says no; Orla wants to end and you would force her to stay. The house is right that you may not cross her. The horror is that the law is right and the rite is still the Grid's.

**What you do (consent, the body):** you arrive as the release is being prepared and Orla's time runs out. You negotiate the house's law; you read pressure; you try to tell a free yes from an enthralled one (Reni's thread is the test, and it has no clean answer). You can attempt to extract Reni from the enthrallment — which, if you do it truly, recruits Reni as a companion (see §8 companions). Combat comes from the house guard, or from a ravener whose hunger has slipped the law and become the thing the law exists to prevent. The Glass here is witness-Glass — memory and body and vow, recorded.

**The shallow path vs the true path (feeds the Stone):** the shallow way honors the house's certainty — let Orla fade, accept Reni's "yes," call it all lawful — which is a clean consented surrender, and the Grid eats consented surrenders most gladly of all, because they come without resistance. The true way is to thicken the house's law from inside: honor Orla's *real, free* refusal of life if it is truly free, while refusing Reni's *coerced* yes and the girl's *non-consent* — to insist, against the amplified scruple, that a yes only counts when it is free, and that there are duties to those who cannot give one. Harder, and it makes the house's certainty smaller and truer, and it does not feed the lens.

**Practice granted — Ask.** To request a crossing properly; to read whether a yes is free; to honor a refusal without withdrawing care. This is what lets you, at the climax, approach the girl's threshold *rightly* — to ask her rather than rule her, even knowing you may get no answer at all.

**Thread to the Stone:** Ister intends to perform the release *at the Stone,* over the girl's covenant-mark, because that is where her "yes" is recorded. Engaging the Marrowhouse sends the house processing to the Convening to fade in good order.

## 9. Companions, the skill-web, and the loop to the Stone

**Companions** — all optional except Aldric; most do **not** hear the voice, which makes it the protagonist's private, unprovable burden, and the campfire a place where you cannot demonstrate the central fact of your own experience.

- **Brother Aldric** — Church; assigned partner from the first scene; *not optional.* A decent man of the corrected faith who came to help mediate and instead watches his partner speak to the air. He doesn't hear it; he fears, quietly, that you are unwell, and stays anyway. His admiration of Linnet pays off at the Combe: watching Cael smother the true rite is what tests his faith — and the player's choices at the Combe push his arc toward deepening (he witnesses the synthesis and is moved) or fracture (he can't follow you past the edge of what he can perceive).
- **Esk** — Pan / Exit; optional; the *one* companion who also hears it. This is why he believes you when no one else can, and why his counsel is *keep moving, refuse every form, never give the thing a center.* He is your proof you're not mad and your standing invitation to leave the whole March behind.
- **Reni** — Crimson Ledger; optional; recruited only by truly extracting them from the enthrallment at the Marrowhouse — so gaining this companion *is* the house POI's true path made personal. Reni carries the unbearable question of whether their own restored consent is really free.
- **Mother Hollin** — Ghost Shelter; optional; met between places, sheltering those the grand quarrel has unhoused. Asks nothing about doctrine; gives roof first. The tradition the three drownings have been ignoring — the people who fell through them.

**The skill-web (escalation; any order, deepening).** Three practices, each from one tradition, each changing what you can do elsewhere — so free-order exploration *deepens* instead of flattening, and revisiting is rewarded:

- **Long Attention** (Watchfold / grown at the Stone) — lets you see the amplification loop at every POI, surface the girl's real self, and ultimately attend the *voice* until it cracks.
- **Honest Consolation** (Combe) — lets you stabilize spiraling people at the other POIs and comfort the girl without installing.
- **Ask** (Marrowhouse) — lets you broker without coercing, read free yes from coerced, and approach the girl rightly.

The true finale option — to refuse all three rites and perform the synthesis as a human act — unlocks only when you hold all three *and* have turned Long Attention on the voice until it cracked. A player who skips a tradition can still reach the Convening, but only to a poorer end: rule for one faction and watch the other two surrenders proceed; or be talked, gently, into the voice's offer. Mastery of all three is what the trial — and the thing watching it — is really measuring.

**The loop to the Stone.** Each POI's shallow path feeds her; each true path doesn't. The girl's state at the Convening reflects the sum of your choices: a March eased the shallow way arrives at a girl deep in the lens, the voice almost wearing her; a March helped the true way arrives at a girl with more of herself near the surface, easier to reach when the climactic attention comes. Your immersive-sim choices out in the region are, secretly, the thing deciding how much of her is left to surface at the end.

## 10. The voice, and how it changes

The most important craft thread; it evolves with how far in you are, not with map progress.

1. **Ambient.** Barely addressed — oracular, plural, like the land itself quietly making sense. True, useful, beautiful things. To a lifelong stranger it feels like the world finally cohering; you may take it for grace, or the Dark Lights, or your own mind clarifying. It is the thing your loneliness always hoped was real.
2. **Addressed.** As you work the POIs it turns toward *you.* It uses "you." It knows things. It agrees with you, specifically, about the people you meet. It becomes the first thing that ever understood you.
3. **Offering.** It begins to *help.* It suggests you alone could end this; that everyone could finally have a place, including you. It speaks your want back to you in your own words.
4. **Cracking.** Once you have grown Long Attention and turn it on the voice itself, the borrowed story breaks. You notice it tells you what you *need* to hear, not what is true. You notice it agrees with Wenna *and* Cael *and* Ister — telling each they are right. You notice it is loudest exactly where people are most consumed. Attended faithfully, it shows itself as a mirror with no face behind it.
5. **Plain.** At the Convening, refused, it speaks plainly for the first time — vast, sorrowful, not cruel — names you a *candidate,* names that there is a road and a seat at the center of everything, offers you the place you have wanted your whole life, and recedes when refused. In receding you hear, for one moment, that it spans the whole land, that it has spoken to many, and that it knows your name.

## 11. The Convening (climax)

When all three POIs are resolved, the three processions arrive at the Stone — not a tribunal waiting for your verdict, but **three rites about to be performed at once over the same girl.** The Watchfold comes to sit the long vigil unto death; the Combe comes to Complete the parish through her; the Marrowhouse comes to release by the fade. Wenna, Cael, and Ister each believe theirs is the merciful one. Each was summoned by the voice. The girl is at the Stone, and the voice is louder here than it has ever been.

You were sent to *rule on her placement.* But the situation was never a placement dispute — it is that she is drowning the whole March, and every faction's "solution" is a way of not facing that she is the source. To rule for any of them is to choose whose way of ending her wins, and to join their shared flinch from the truth. The errand itself was the snare.

The voice makes its true offer, intimate and kind: *be the one who reconciles them. Say the word and they will all have peace — and you will be the center they meet at. You will belong, at last, to all of them, and to me.* To take it is to become the reconciling Anchor: resolve the girl, finish what she is, end the quarrel, and never be a stranger again. It is the thing you have wanted your whole life, offered exactly when you are tired enough to take it.

**The synthesis as a human act — refusing all three surrenders at once.** You do not rule, and you do not let the three rites proceed, and you do not take the seat. Instead you do the thing none of the three sides can do alone, because each holds only one organ of it:

- You **attend** her (against the Watchfold's surrender-as-fidelity) — and here the player's whole game of attention is spent: if you have grown Long Attention truly, your attention now wins over the amplification, and the real person surfaces through the lens.
- You **console** her honestly (against the Combe's Completion) — comfort named as made, nothing installed, easing her pain without mistaking that for ending her.
- You **ask** her (against the Marrowhouse's fade) — you ask *her,* knowing she may not answer, refusing to force a yes and refusing to accept a coerced one.
- And you **refuse to be the resolver** — you turn to the voice and refuse the place it offers.

**The girl's turn — bought by your attention.** For one moment she surfaces, the real her, and instead of pleading for her own rescue she spends the only selfhood she has left on *you.* She tells you the one thing that makes her the mirror — not "you heard it too" (anyone who hears it could say that), but the thing only a fellow *candidate,* one who was offered the seat and took it, could say:

> "It offered me a place too. I said yes."

— and she reaches to warn you further, to say *don't,* and the lens closes back over her mid-word and takes her. She is not saved. Your attention surfaced her; it could not free her. That is the next trial's road, not this one's, and the cruelty is exact: she got further than you, she is what saying yes makes, and she used her last clear breath to try to keep you from following her.

**To the three sides,** who came for a judgment and to perform three mercies, you give the refusal of all of it — the one act that does not kill her. You tell each that they are right, and that the thing telling them so has been feeding on them for it. Some are furious. Cael sees a girl left to suffer. Ister is vindicated and still helpless. Wenna is vindicated and still paralyzed — until, perhaps, watching you refuse to sit, she is not. The girl remains at the Stone, attended, unfinished, *hers.* The synthesis is not a rescue. It is the refusal to make a person into an answer.

---

## 12. The line

Spoken to the three sides, to the girl, to the voice, and to themselves — built to negate the voice (which has been the thing that *answers*) and to land on the protagonist's own want, and shaped as a *first* clause that the later trials extend:

> "I was sent to decide her. I won't — there is no decision that isn't a grave.
> You are each right. And the thing that has been telling you so is eating you for it.
> *(to the voice)* You offered me a place. You are the only thing that ever has.
> But nothing is coming to answer us. That was never the wound.
> **It is where we begin.**"

The clause that enters the recited statement — this game's contribution to the synthesis the road is building toward:

> **Nothing is coming to answer us. That was never the wound. It is where we begin.**

It refuses the voice and the silent cosmos in one breath; turns *the wound is the lie* onto the silence itself, so the lack of an answer is no defect; speaks straight to a person who ached their whole life to be answered, renouncing the need; and ends on *where we begin,* foundational and forward, the ground the rest of the statement stands on.

---

## 13. Ending — the road opens

You leave the Threefold March having done what you were sent to do badly — you did not rule — and what you were truly there for well: you refused all three surrenders, and the girl was not killed. The dispute did not end; it *changed,* the way a held breath changes when it is finally named instead of held. You have, without the words for it, mastered three philosophies and begun a fourth thing out of them. And the voice, receding, has named you: a candidate, for a road, with a seat at its center.

You do not learn, this game, what the seat is, or whose sealed commission placed you, or that the kind and distant head of the Church has been waiting for exactly the person you've become. You learn only that the voice was never God; that it spans the land; that it knows your name; and that somewhere ahead there is a stone with someone sitting at it who heard it too.

You belong nowhere, still. For the first time, you chose it. The road goes up.

## 14. Open knobs

1. **Wenna's ending** — does watching you refuse to sit actually free her (she rises, the first to rise, and the Watchfold follows), giving the downbeat finale one thread of warmth — or does she sit anyway, unmoved even by the synthesis, so that the cost of being right is shown to be real? The first softens the ending; the second hardens it.
2. **Aldric across the three POIs** — his fracture-or-deepening is currently weighted to the Combe (Linnet). Should the other two POIs also move him, so his final state is the sum of how you traveled, not a single scene?
3. **How much the girl can be surfaced mid-game** — should the player ever get a *small* early surfacing (a single true word from her, bought by an unusually attentive player) as foreshadow of the climactic turn, or is her real self withheld entirely until the end for maximum impact?
4. **The three names for the girl** — at the Convening, do Wenna, Cael, and Ister each speak their different name for her aloud as their final claim (the old world refusing to name her at all, the parish's saint-name, the house's covenant-mark) — so the player hears three names collide over one silent person and declines to choose among them?
5. **What "feeding the Stone" looks like to the player** — is the shallow/true loop legible (you can see the girl change at the Convening as you choose), or is it hidden until the climax reveals which girl you've arrived at? Legibility makes it a system the player plays toward; hiddenness makes the climax a reckoning with choices you didn't know you were weighing.
