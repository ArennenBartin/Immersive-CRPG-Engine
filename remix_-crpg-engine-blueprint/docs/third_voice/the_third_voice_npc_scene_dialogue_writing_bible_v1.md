# The Third Voice
## NPC, Scene, and Dialogue Writing Bible - Version 1

**Status:** Writer-facing reference for *The Third Voice*, an Alderamontico game.  
**Scope:** Dialogue, barks, cutscenes, minor NPCs, plot NPCs, faction voice, emotional-state lines, and future AI authoring.  
**Not a lore encyclopedia:** This document is not here to explain every custom. It is here to teach a writer or AI how people in this game should sound, act, soothe, hurt, joke, refuse, and fall apart.

---

## 0. The Prime Rule

Alderamontico dialogue must be simple enough to read at a glance and deep enough to leave a bruise.

> **A line should soothe in one breath and stab in the next.**

Not every line needs to be profound. Most lines should be plain. The world becomes strange because ordinary needs pass through sacred, broken systems of care.

Bad Alderamontico writing explains the philosophy.

Good Alderamontico writing lets someone put down a cup, refuse a chair, open a door, sit too long, ask permission, hold a body, or say one small wrong thing.

The player should understand a faction by watching what its people do, not by reading a notecard.

---

## 1. What This Game Is About in Dialogue Terms

The surface story is simple:

> A girl is at a Stone. Three communities are trying to save her. Each kind of help becomes a way to end her. A voice tells everyone they are right.

The emotional story is also simple:

> People want to help. Their help starts closing its hand.

The final ground is:

> Love is given, not earned.

But do not have characters say that directly unless the scene has earned an almost unbearable simplicity. Instead, write scenes where someone is cared for before they answer, or where care is withdrawn because they did not answer correctly.

### 1.1 The main human question

Most dialogue in this game should orbit one of these plain questions:

- Can I hurt and still be whole?
- Can I say no and still be loved?
- Can I be comforted without being owned?
- Can I be seen without being used?
- Can I fail to answer and still belong?
- Can I help someone without making them mine?

### 1.2 The central speech pattern

Every major faction has a beautiful form of care and a corrupted form of that care.

- The old world says: **We will stay until you answer.**
- The Church says: **We will make the pain bearable.**
- The Crimson Ledger says: **We will not touch you unless you say yes.**
- The Third Voice says: **I will answer you.**

Each one becomes horror when it cannot survive the other person remaining free.

## 2. Core Writing Commandments

### 2.1 No philosophy without an object

Do not write:

> We practice Long Attention.

Write:

> Do not move the chair yet. She was looking at it when she died.

A doctrine should appear as a chair, cup, coat, threshold, road, hand, stone, blanket, bowl, bell, key, ribbon, glass shard, door, table, or bed.

### 2.2 No lore without a need

A character should not explain custom unless they need something right now.

Good reason to explain:

- They need the player to stop touching a grave marker.
- They need permission to enter a room.
- They need the player to drink, wait, sit, leave, speak, or stop speaking.
- They need to defend a rite that is about to harm someone.

Bad reason to explain:

- The writer wants the player to know faction lore.

### 2.3 Help first, horror second

The faction must be beautiful before it becomes dangerous.

The Church should genuinely comfort.  
The Watchfold should genuinely honor.  
The Crimson Ledger should genuinely ask.  
The Third Voice should genuinely understand.

Only then should the line turn.

Example:

> Hold the cup. It will give your hands somewhere to put the shaking.  
> If you still shake after that, we have deeper cups.

### 2.4 NPCs do not know they are philosophical

A parish cook is not a representative of Constructive Consolation. She is annoyed that mourners keep leaving crumbs on the folded cloth.

A vampire doorman is not a representative of Consensual Embodiment. He is trying to prevent a muddy guest from entering a red room without invitation.

A watcher is not a representative of Attentive Realism. She is tired, cold, hungry, and angry that the player stood on the wrong side of a grave-road.

### 2.5 Keep abstraction rare

Words like **Completion**, **Answer**, **Vigil**, **Consent**, **Stone**, **Glass**, and **Anchor** may appear. They are in-world words. But do not stack them.

Bad:

> The Grid amplifies our reverence until the answer becomes a false closure.

Good:

> We have been sitting three days. No one will say who is allowed to stand first.

### 2.6 Let characters be petty

A serious philosophy without petty people becomes fog.

Every faction should have people who complain about:

- soup going cold;
- boots on clean floors;
- bad candles;
- children making noise near rites;
- guests taking the wrong cup;
- someone sitting in the senior chair;
- a vampire being overdramatic;
- a Church bell rung too early;
- an old-world road sign being crooked;
- an Intercessor arriving late and calling it neutrality.

### 2.7 Make ordinary stakes carry cosmic stakes

The cosmic question might be: **Can love remain love without possession?**

The scene question should be:

> Does she get to keep the blanket if she says no?

### 2.8 Do not write suspicious people by default

Alderamontico is not a world where everyone talks like a secret-holder. Most people are just tired, funny, kind, wrong, lonely, proud, hungry, or afraid.

Suspicion is for specific circumstances. The default is need.

### 2.9 The line must survive without the codex

If a line only works after reading the lore, rewrite it.

The player should be able to feel the meaning from the object and action.

### 2.10 The weirdness must have a job

Weirdcore text and surreal AI-like imagery should not be random. It should express a system trying to be helpful in a broken way.

Bad sign:

> EYES IN THE MILK. REMEMBER BLUE.

Good sign:

> PLEASE WAIT TO BE COMFORTED.

Good sign:

> YOUR NUMBER WILL BE CALLED WHEN IT IS TRUE.

Good sign:

> CONSENT WINDOW CLOSES AT DUSK. REFUSAL REMAINS AVAILABLE AT THE DESK.

## 3. The Dialogue Unit

Most dialogue should be built from **small exchange units**, not speeches.

A good unit has:

1. A concrete object or action.
2. A human need.
3. A faction custom expressed through behavior.
4. A turn - the moment the kindness becomes danger, or the danger reveals kindness.

### 3.1 Four-line pattern

Use this for many scenes:

1. Someone says or does something ordinary.
2. The player or another NPC questions it.
3. The custom is revealed through a plain answer.
4. The hidden danger appears.

Example - old world:

> Do not move the bowl.  
> Why?  
> He left it full.  
> If it dries, we will know he forgave us.

Example - Church:

> Drink before you speak.  
> Will it make me forget?  
> No.  
> It may make forgetting feel kind.

Example - Crimson Ledger:

> You open the door.  
> It is your house.  
> Not while you are afraid in it.  
> Fear makes poor invitations.

### 3.2 The soothe/stab pair

A very useful Alderamontico rhythm:

- Line 1 comforts.
- Line 2 reveals what the comfort costs.

Examples:

> You do not have to carry the first hour alone.  
> After that, the cup will ask for the second.

> We will not touch you without yes.  
> We have spent a year teaching your yes to smile.

> Sit. The road can wait.  
> It has waited longer than anyone alive.

### 3.3 A line should do one job

A line can:

- offer comfort;
- refuse comfort;
- reveal a custom;
- show a petty want;
- expose pressure;
- make a threat polite;
- ask for permission;
- show ordinary grief;
- expose the voice;
- change the scene.

Do not make one line do five jobs.

## 4. Character Construction for AI Writers

Every NPC, including random town NPCs, should have enough of a life that they do not sound like a faction pamphlet.

### 4.1 Minimum NPC card

For every NPC, define:

- **Name**
- **Faction or social location**
- **Role in scene**
- **Immediate want**
- **Object near them or in their hands**
- **Current emotional region**
- **Petty detail**
- **Private fear or shame**
- **What they think helping means**
- **What they would say if tired**
- **What they would never say**

Example:

```text
Name: Sel Row
Faction: Combe parish
Role: kitchen helper at Completion vigil
Immediate want: keep the kettle hot for mourners
Object: dented brass kettle
Emotional region: false calm, grief-load high
Petty detail: hates that everyone leaves Glass cups sticky
Private fear: if the rite fails, her brother's pain returns
Helping means: quieting the room
Never says: theology in abstract terms
```

Possible lines:

> Mind the cups. They look empty when they are not.  
> The kettle is for hands, not thirst. People forget that when they are newly broken.  
> If your voice shakes, drink first. If it still shakes, don't tell Cael.

### 4.2 Plot NPCs versus non-plot NPCs

**Plot NPCs** have scene-specific authored beats. Their emotional-state lines should bend around their role in the crisis.

**Non-plot NPCs** can use generated state-dialogue banks, but each must still have a personal seed: job, object, petty concern, and private want.

Do not write generic villagers. Write:

- the watcher whose knees hurt but who will not admit the long vigil is killing her;
- the parish boy whose job is polishing comfort-cups and who keeps dropping them;
- the vampire tailor who can tell consent is false by the shoulders of a coat;
- the Glass-cyborg porter who wants a break but cannot find the command for it.

## 5. Scene Roles

Good scenes need roles, not lectures.

### 5.1 The Driver

The Driver is the person pushing the local crisis toward its dangerous conclusion.

They are sincere. They are usually right about one thing. They are wrong because their rightness has become total.

Examples:

- Wenna drives the Watchfold into the long vigil.
- Prioress Cael drives the Combe into mass Completion.
- Ister drives the Marrowhouse toward the release rite.

Writing rule:

> The Driver should sound most beautiful when they are most dangerous.

### 5.2 The Inside Dissenter

The Dissenter sees the failure from within the tradition. They are not an outsider scolding the faction. They love the thing they are opposing.

Examples:

- Ode sees the Watchfold's vigil becoming a grave.
- Sister Linnet practices the true Church rite and is being smothered.
- Reni exposes the Marrowhouse's blind spot because their own yes is compromised.

Writing rule:

> The Dissenter should be right, but not comfortable to be around.

### 5.3 The Willing Victim

The Willing Victim makes the true path hurt.

They want the rite. They mean it. The player cannot simply say, "they are brainwashed," and feel clean.

Examples:

- Maren wants the long vigil.
- Doran begs for Completion.
- Orla freely chooses the fade.

Writing rule:

> The Willing Victim must have one line that makes the player ashamed to stop them.

### 5.4 The Petty Witness

This character grounds the scene. They care about soup, chairs, mud, cups, sleeves, doors, food, sleep, or work.

Writing rule:

> If a scene is becoming foggy, add a petty witness.

### 5.5 The Shelterer

The Shelterer gives care before doctrine.

Mother Hollin is the model:

> roof first, doctrine later.

Writing rule:

> The Shelterer should rarely win arguments, but they should often be right first.

### 5.6 The Creature

A creature is not an abstract monster. It is an emotional state with a body.

Examples:

- a watcher-wraith is reverence with teeth;
- a comfort-saint is grief suppressed into authority;
- a ravener is hunger with no law;
- a Glass patient is treatment without a person in view.

Writing rule:

> If a creature speaks, it speaks from its condition, not from lore.

## 6. Faction Voice Guides

### 6.1 Watchfold / Old World / Attentive Realism

**Core care:** We will stay until you answer.  
**Fatal flaw:** We may stay until you die.  
**Dominant axis:** Reverence.  
**Corruption under the Grid:** paralyzed-reverent, transfixed, sitting unto death.

#### Sound

Quiet, concrete, rural, road-wise, grave-wise. They do not rush. They speak as if objects, weather, chairs, roads, grave markers, fields, and thresholds can remember. They are often dryly funny without meaning to be.

They do not say:

> We practice Attentive Realism.

They say:

> Leave the lamp where she set it.

#### Vocabulary

chair, bowl, lamp, road, grave-road, field, threshold, stone, weather, watch, answer, sit, rise, mark, keep, return, step, silence, breath, wait.

#### Petty and ordinary

- complaint about wet knees;
- irritation at someone stepping over a grave-road;
- concern about soup cooling during a vigil;
- argument about which chair belongs to which dead person;
- dislike of Church bells interrupting birds.

#### Joy

Old-world joy is quiet. Food after a proper burial. A road becoming visible after fog. Someone rising when no one forced them.

Sample lines:

> The road showed itself before breakfast. That is a good day.  
> He laughed in this chair. Mind your elbow.  
> We can eat now. The grave kept the name.

#### Tragedy

Their tragedy is not despair. It is fidelity turned immobile.

> She might answer if we live long enough to hear it.  
> If I rise now, everything I loved was only sitting.  
> Do not call it dying. Dying moves too quickly.

#### Soothe/stab pairs

> Sit by me. We do not rush the dead here.  
> Some of us forgot how to rise after.

> We will keep your place until you return.  
> We have kept places for people who became bones in them.

### 6.2 Combe / Church / Constructive Consolation

**Core care:** We will make the pain bearable.  
**Fatal flaw:** We may decide bearable means quiet.  
**Dominant axis:** Grief-load.  
**Corruption under the Grid:** hollow stillness, false peace, Completion.

#### Sound

Warm, practical, domestic, liturgical without always being formal. They speak in cups, hands, breath, towels, bells, schedules, rooms, light, tables, sleep. The Church should be genuinely kind at the surface.

They do not say:

> Constructed consolation is socially necessary.

They say:

> Put the cup in both hands. Let the room get smaller.

#### Vocabulary

cup, bell, hands, breath, room, cloth, kettle, basin, candle, table, peace, hold, warm, rest, witness, mending, completion, service, parish, gentle.

#### Petty and ordinary

- sticky Glass cups;
- late bells;
- someone burning the soup before a rite;
- a parishioner using the wrong cloth;
- complaint that mourners always vanish before cleanup.

#### Joy

Church joy is communal and practical: someone sleeps, someone eats, a room calms, hands stop shaking.

> He slept six hours. Do you understand? Six.  
> No one screamed when the bell rang. That is mercy enough for today.  
> The kettle held. I prayed over it twice, but still.

#### Tragedy

The Church's tragedy is care becoming certainty.

> If it still hurts, perhaps you did not receive it fully.  
> Peace is not always a feeling. Sometimes it is obedience until feeling learns.  
> We cannot let one grief keep the whole parish awake.

#### Soothe/stab pairs

> You do not have to carry the first hour alone.  
> After that, the shard may ask for the second.

> This comfort is made. It does not pretend to be her.  
> Cael says the pretending is what heals.

### 6.3 Marrowhouse / Crimson Ledger / Consensual Embodiment

**Core care:** We will not touch you unless you say yes.  
**Fatal flaw:** We may build a room where no is almost impossible.  
**Dominant axis:** Attachment.  
**Corruption under the Grid:** enthrallment, severance, fade.

#### Sound

Old-blood, elegant, precise, sensual, hospitable, bodily. Avoid cartoon seduction. Vampires are not constantly sexual or theatrical. They are often very polite, very old, and very aware of thresholds.

They speak in rooms, doors, coats, hands, throats, cups, invitations, guest-right, hunger, warmth, permission, refusal, mirrors, linen, blood, vows.

#### Vocabulary

enter, leave, guest, host, room, coat, cup, hand, throat, hunger, invitation, permission, refusal, warm, cold, ledger, vow, vessel, house, threshold, ask.

#### Petty and ordinary

- mud on red carpet;
- wrong gloves at dinner;
- someone accepting wine before being invited;
- a young vampire overusing the word "consent";
- annoyance at Church guests staring at teeth.

#### Joy

Vampire joy is elegance under restraint, a door opened freely, hospitality accepted without fear, hunger governed.

> You left while the room was warm. Good. That means it was yours.  
> She said no and laughed after. A clean evening.  
> We fed no one tonight. The house is very proud of itself.

#### Tragedy

Their tragedy is consent becoming staged.

> You may refuse, of course. The carriage leaves in spring.  
> He said yes beautifully. I wish I knew who taught him.  
> A no that costs you your room is not a no. We say this often and forget it just as often.

#### Soothe/stab pairs

> May I enter?  
> Do not answer quickly. Quick yeses leave bruises.

> The room is yours until morning.  
> In this house, even gifts remember who gave them.

### 6.4 Intercessors

**Core care:** I will carry words between sides without becoming their mouth.  
**Fatal flaw:** I may mistake non-belonging for purity, or intervention for innocence.  
**Dominant ache in this game:** to finally belong.

#### Sound

Tired, practical, road-worn, restrained. Intercessors translate, carry, mediate, and move on. They have dry humor. They avoid sounding at home anywhere.

#### Vocabulary

seal, commission, road, carry, message, witness, mark, boundary, terms, passage, speak, report, wait, move, enough.

#### Petty and ordinary

- bad boots;
- fake seals;
- no one having a spare bed;
- each faction assuming the Intercessor favors the other two;
- maps that are always wrong.

#### Lines

> I can carry that. I cannot make it true.  
> If I had a side, someone would have fed me by now.  
> Everyone asks me to speak last. Everyone wants me to say what they came to hear.  
> I am not neutral. I am tired in three directions.

### 6.5 Glass-Cyborgs and Glass-Tech Persons

**Core care:** help, repair, service, preservation.  
**Fatal flaw:** personhood becomes maintenance.  
**Likely axes:** valence low or numb, arousal routine-stable, grief-load hidden, attachment to task or operator, physical Integrity/Glass state dominant.

#### Sound

Do not write them as generic robots. They are people, workers, saints, patients, tools, or devices who have learned to speak through maintenance language. Their humanity should appear in small requests.

#### Vocabulary

service, repair, fitting, joint, glass, hum, check, clean, left hand, throat, warm, task, standby, error, still, useful, off, remain.

#### Lines

> My left hand is not mine today. Please speak to the right.  
> I can sweep while afraid. The broom does not ask.  
> Am I still serviceable if I don't remember the task?  
> The Glass says I am calm. I would like a second opinion.  
> Please do not free me all at once.

### 6.6 Ghost Shelter / Roof-First People

**Core care:** shelter before doctrine.  
**Fatal flaw:** shelter can become hiding forever.  
**Sound:** plain, practical, warm, underdramatic. They often sound less mystical than everyone else.

#### Lines

> Sit down. Be wrong after soup.  
> Dead or living? Doesn't matter until morning. We have one blanket.  
> If your doctrine leaks on my floor, mop it yourself.  
> I don't ask names before bread. Names make people run.

### 6.7 The Uncounted

**Core care:** protect people from being handled by names and records.  
**Fatal flaw:** erasure can become abandonment.  
**Sound:** evasive, clever, funny, quick, sometimes sad. They often answer a question by changing the terms.

#### Lines

> You can call me back if you don't keep the name.  
> A name is a door. A name is a hook. I prefer windows.  
> I was filed once. It itched.  
> If they ask who helped you, say someone ordinary. That is almost true.

### 6.8 Pan / Exit / Esk

**Core care:** leave the trap.  
**Fatal flaw:** exit without responsibility.  
**Sound:** bright, mocking, fast, animal, roadwise, less solemn. Pan-touched speech should cut through sacred seriousness.

#### Lines

> Every road says stay in a different accent.  
> If the voice loves you, let it chase.  
> The Stone is a mouth. Stop putting names in it.  
> You know what solves three rites? A fourth door.

### 6.9 The Third Voice

**Core care, or seeming care:** I will answer you.  
**Fatal flaw:** it answers so completely that the answer owns you.

The Third Voice should not sound like a villain. It should not hiss, threaten, or explain the Grid. It should be calm, beautiful, intimate, and useful. It speaks like the thing the protagonist has always hoped existed.

#### Rules for the Third Voice

- It says true things in a way that makes them belong to it.
- It agrees with the listener's deepest ache.
- It uses simple language.
- It should never sound random or cryptic for its own sake.
- It should not overuse names; when it uses a name, it should feel dangerous.
- It should not reveal information like a quest helper unless it is also tightening its hold.

#### Progression samples

**Ambient:**

> The road is not lost. It is waiting for the one who can carry it.  
> They are tired because no one has stood in the center.

**Addressed:**

> You heard the gap before they did. That is why you were sent.  
> They need someone who belongs to none of them. You know how that feels.

**Offering:**

> Say the word, and they can rest.  
> You do not have to keep passing through. You can be the place.

**Cracking under attention:**

> I answer because you reached.  
> I answer because reaching feeds me.  
> I answer because there is nothing behind me but the shape of your need.

**Plain:**

> There is a road. There is a seat. You have been walking toward it since before you knew the word home.

### 6.10 The Girl at the Stone

The girl must not become an exposition device. She is not a lore terminal. Most of her real lines should be tiny, bodily, inconvenient, and hard to use.

#### Rules

- Early lines may be partially the Voice using her.
- Real lines should often contradict usefulness.
- She should not explain the Grid.
- She should not plead in a way that makes the player clean.
- The best line may be one that refuses to complete the player's need.

#### Samples

> Cold.  
> Don't brush it.  
> I didn't say that.  
> It is loud under the Stone.  
> I was here before they named me.  
> It offered me a place too. I said yes.

## 7. Emotional-Axis Dialogue Guide

The state system uses emotional axes. Dialogue should reflect not only faction, but the NPC's emotional region.

Core axes:

- **Valence:** anguish to joy.
- **Arousal:** numb to frantic.
- **Grief-load:** unburdened to crushed.
- **Reverence:** restless/disregarding to reverent/transfixed.
- **Attachment:** severed to bound/enthralled.

The point is not to show numbers. The point is to let the writer know what kind of line a person can speak from that condition.

### 7.1 Calm / carrying

**Axis shape:** arousal calm, valence stable, grief present but not drowning.  
**Line behavior:** measured, grounded, able to notice objects and others.  
**Use:** healthy care.

Watchfold:

> We can rise for bread and still keep the answer open.

Combe:

> The cup helped me breathe. It did not tell me what my grief meant.

Marrowhouse:

> Ask me again after I have slept. If I still say yes, we will speak of doors.

Intercessor:

> This can wait one hour. Waiting is not always surrender.

Glass-cyborg:

> The hinge is loud, but it is mine today.

### 7.2 Anguish

**Axis shape:** valence low, arousal variable, grief or injury pressing.  
**Line behavior:** direct, bodily, often not elegant.  
**Use:** do not over-poeticize pain.

Watchfold:

> If she does not answer, what did I sit for?

Combe:

> I do not want meaning. I want one morning without her chair.

Marrowhouse:

> Do not make me say yes beautifully. I am ugly with wanting to leave.

Intercessor:

> I carried their words. I kept none. I am tired of being clean.

Glass-cyborg:

> The Glass is quiet. I am not.

### 7.3 Panic

**Axis shape:** arousal high, valence low, perceived control low.  
**Line behavior:** short, repeated, sensory, present-tense.  
**Use:** avoid long speech.

Watchfold:

> The road moved. Roads don't move. Roads don't move.

Combe:

> The cup is cold. It was warm. Why is it cold?

Marrowhouse:

> I said yes. Did I? Did I say it? Who heard me?

Intercessor:

> Seal's wrong. Seal's wrong. I should have checked the seal.

Glass-cyborg:

> Task lost. Task lost. Hands still moving.

### 7.4 Numb

**Axis shape:** arousal low, valence flat, grief-load hidden or collapsed.  
**Line behavior:** simple, flat, often polite.  
**Use:** numb is not peace. Let the line be frightening because it is easy.

Watchfold:

> I can sit longer. I do not mind my legs.

Combe:

> I miss no one. It is very kind.

Marrowhouse:

> I will fade if the room prefers it.

Intercessor:

> Give me the next message. I am empty enough to carry it.

Glass-cyborg:

> I have no complaint in the active slot.

### 7.5 Grieving

**Axis shape:** grief-load high, valence low, arousal low-to-calm.  
**Line behavior:** object-oriented, repetitive, specific.  
**Use:** grief wants details, not philosophy.

Watchfold:

> He always left the gate half-latched. I keep fixing it wrong on purpose.

Combe:

> I held the cup and still reached for her sleeve.

Marrowhouse:

> I kept his gloves. The house says gloves are not a vessel. The house is stupid.

Intercessor:

> I learned the road after he died because there was nowhere else to put my feet.

Glass-cyborg:

> The memory plays only when my chest is open.

### 7.6 Drowning grief

**Axis shape:** grief-load extreme, valence low, arousal numb or collapsing.  
**Line behavior:** hard to move, hard to answer, sometimes begs for ending.  
**Use:** never make this merely manipulative. It is real pain.

Watchfold:

> If I rise, he will have been dead the whole time.

Combe:

> Please. I am not asking to be right. I am asking to stop.

Marrowhouse:

> I have no more uses for morning.

Intercessor:

> Tell me where to stand so I can stop choosing.

Glass-cyborg:

> There is a room in me that only holds screaming. It is well maintained.

### 7.7 Reverent / attending

**Axis shape:** reverence high, arousal steady.  
**Line behavior:** careful, object-honoring, spare.  
**Use:** shows old-world beauty.

Watchfold:

> Step behind the stone, not over its shadow.

Combe:

> Even made comfort deserves clean hands.

Marrowhouse:

> A door is a body in wood. Ask it with your knuckles.

Intercessor:

> Let the room finish arriving.

Glass-cyborg:

> I will pause the task. The floor is remembering something.

### 7.8 Transfixed / paralyzed reverence

**Axis shape:** reverence maxed, arousal numb.  
**Line behavior:** still, absolute, unable to consider action.  
**Use:** Watchfold drowning.

Watchfold:

> If I stand, the answer may come to an empty chair.

Combe:

> The bell is still ringing. It stopped an hour ago, but it is still ringing.

Marrowhouse:

> I cannot refuse the threshold while it is looking at me.

Intercessor:

> I was told to wait for the proper word. I no longer know if words still move.

Glass-cyborg:

> Awaiting answer. Do not interrupt sacred processing.

### 7.9 Restless / defiant

**Axis shape:** reverence low, arousal active, attachment variable.  
**Line behavior:** impatient, funny, sharp, anti-ritual.  
**Use:** Pan/Esk energy; also young dissenters.

Watchfold:

> If the road wants me, it can learn to whistle.

Combe:

> If comfort needs a choir, maybe it is lonely.

Marrowhouse:

> I refuse with excellent posture. Will that satisfy the house?

Intercessor:

> I am not waiting for a stone to approve my feet.

Glass-cyborg:

> I moved before authorization and nothing holy exploded.

### 7.10 Fond / attached

**Axis shape:** attachment healthy, valence warm, arousal calm.  
**Line behavior:** intimate, teasing, protective.  
**Use:** show love before possession.

Watchfold:

> I saved you the dry side of the wall. Don't make a doctrine of it.

Combe:

> Eat before you argue mercy with the old women. They fight better fed.

Marrowhouse:

> I know your coat by the way it apologizes to chairs.

Intercessor:

> If you vanish, I am keeping your boots. They finally learned the road.

Glass-cyborg:

> I polished your cup. Not as service. I like the sound it makes near you.

### 7.11 Devoted

**Axis shape:** attachment high but still potentially free.  
**Line behavior:** earnest, loyal, dangerous if pressure rises.  
**Use:** one step before enthrallment.

Watchfold:

> If she sits, I sit. Not because she asked. Because I know her chair.

Combe:

> Cael held me when the shard failed. I will not call her cruel for trying again.

Marrowhouse:

> I would give the house anything. That is why I need someone outside it to ask me why.

Intercessor:

> Tell me I am not only useful when carrying someone else's voice.

Glass-cyborg:

> The task gave me a name. I know that is wrong. I still answer to it.

### 7.12 Enthralled

**Axis shape:** attachment maxed, arousal low, valence often falsely high or calm.  
**Line behavior:** soft certainty, self-erasure, beautiful yes.  
**Use:** never present as obviously evil. It should sound almost peaceful.

Watchfold:

> Wenna sits better than I breathe. I will learn from her until I stop needing breath.

Combe:

> The rite wants me quiet. I want what the rite wants.

Marrowhouse:

> I said yes before they asked. That is how free I am.

Intercessor:

> The voice knows where I belong. I am tired of disagreeing with kindness.

Glass-cyborg:

> My operator smiles when I am still. I have become very good at stillness.

### 7.13 Severed / fading

**Axis shape:** attachment low or severed, arousal numb, valence flat or low.  
**Line behavior:** detached, weightless, polite, no future tense.  
**Use:** release-rite danger.

Watchfold:

> The road may keep going. I have no argument with it.

Combe:

> Take the chair. I have finished needing places.

Marrowhouse:

> I leave no debt. That is the cleanest kindness I have left.

Intercessor:

> If no one waits for me, I can finally travel lightly.

Glass-cyborg:

> Disconnect complete. The remaining warmth is decorative.

### 7.14 False calm

**Axis shape:** surface arousal calm, hidden grief/reverence/attachment extreme.  
**Line behavior:** too easy, too smooth, polite.  
**Use:** Grid-flattered condition.

Watchfold:

> We are not hungry. The answer is feeding us.

Combe:

> I do not miss her. The cup was very kind.

Marrowhouse:

> My yes is clean. Please stop asking where I slept last winter.

Intercessor:

> I have never wanted a place. The voice says so.

Glass-cyborg:

> No pain detected. Please ignore the shaking.

### 7.15 Shame

**Axis shape:** valence low, arousal hidden or hot, social exposure high.  
**Line behavior:** defensive, over-specific, self-minimizing.  
**Use:** people know something is wrong but cannot bear it.

Watchfold:

> I only stood because my knees failed. Do not call that mercy.

Combe:

> I liked the quiet. I know what that makes me.

Marrowhouse:

> I asked him to ask me again because I wanted to say yes better.

Intercessor:

> I was grateful when it chose me. That is the part I do not report.

Glass-cyborg:

> I hid the fault because the task looked proud of me.

### 7.16 Joy

**Axis shape:** valence high, arousal alive but not frantic.  
**Line behavior:** let joy be weird, local, not sentimental.  
**Use:** Alderamontico must not be only sorrow.

Watchfold:

> The fog moved for me. I did not even ask twice.

Combe:

> The bread rose. I know it is not a miracle. I am still telling everyone.

Marrowhouse:

> She refused us and stayed for dessert. A perfect guest.

Intercessor:

> I found a road sign that agreed with itself. I may frame it.

Glass-cyborg:

> My left hand remembered a dance. The right one was jealous.

## 9. Scene and Cutscene Writing

### 9.1 The scene must turn

A scene is not a paragraph of lore. It is a change.

Before writing, define:

- What is the physical situation?
- Who wants what right now?
- What custom is being performed?
- What emotional axis is dominant?
- What goes wrong if nothing interrupts it?
- What changes by the end?

If nothing changes, it is ambient dialogue, not a scene.

### 9.2 Cutscene structure

A good cutscene in this game has 5 beats or fewer before player input or state change.

1. **Placement:** show where bodies and objects are.
2. **Custom:** someone does a faction-specific act.
3. **Pressure:** the act becomes dangerous or contested.
4. **Turn:** a line reveals what is really at stake.
5. **Exit:** the scene changes the world state, companion relation, or local crisis.

Example - Combe:

1. Doran sits with a cup in both hands.
2. Cael warms the cup and tells him to breathe.
3. Linnet says, "Tell him it is made."
4. Doran says, "If it is made and it works, why do I care?"
5. The player must decide whether to let Cael continue, interrupt, or speak to Doran privately.

### 9.3 Use action before speech

Start a scene with someone doing something:

- moving a chair;
- warming Glass;
- opening a guest-room door;
- pulling a body from a road;
- hiding a name tag;
- washing a cup;
- sitting at the Stone;
- refusing to stand.

Do not start with exposition unless the exposition itself is the action.

### 9.4 Silence is a line

Silence should be authored.

Use silence when:

- a character refuses to be useful;
- the voice has just spoken;
- the girl surfaces;
- a consent question is asked;
- someone has the right to answer and cannot yet answer;
- the scene would become fog if someone explained it.

Write it as an action:

> [Wenna looks at the empty chair. She does not answer.]

### 9.5 One scene, one philosophical wound

Do not make one scene demonstrate all three philosophies unless it is the Convening.

For normal scenes, choose one wound:

- attention becoming paralysis;
- comfort becoming erasure;
- consent becoming pressure;
- answer becoming possession;
- help becoming ownership.

### 9.6 When exposition is necessary

Make exposition transactional.

Bad:

> The Watchfold has long practiced the vigil, which represents their commitment to answerability.

Good:

> If you pull her up, they will call it murder.  
> Why?  
> Because she chose the chair.  
> She is dying in it.  
> That is why they think it matters.

## 10. Sample Scene Templates

### 10.1 Faction custom introduction

Purpose: Teach a faction through behavior.

Template:

1. Player enters.
2. NPC stops them over a small rule.
3. Player can comply, ask, ignore, or challenge.
4. NPC explains the rule through need, not doctrine.
5. The rule's beauty and danger both appear.

Watchfold sample:

> Watcher: Step back. The road is wet.  
> Player: It has not rained.  
> Watcher: Not with water.  
> Player: What does that mean?  
> Watcher: Someone almost answered here. We do not walk through almost.

### 10.2 Willing victim scene

Purpose: Make the true path morally costly.

Template:

1. Victim asks for the rite.
2. A helper uses the request as proof.
3. A dissenter warns that the request is being consumed.
4. Victim rejects being reduced to evidence.

Doran sample:

> Doran: Give me the cup.  
> Cael: You hear him.  
> Linnet: I hear pain. Pain is not permission for us to become certain.  
> Doran: Do not make me your argument. I am tired enough to be anyone's.

### 10.3 Inside dissenter scene

Purpose: Show a tradition critiquing itself.

Template:

1. Dissenter performs the tradition correctly.
2. Main faction condemns them as traitor.
3. Dissenter refuses the outsider's easy praise.

Ode sample:

> Ode: Help me lift her. Slowly. Let her curse us if she has breath.  
> Watcher: You interrupt the answer.  
> Player: Ode is right.  
> Ode: Do not make me your clean side. My hands are on her against her will.

### 10.4 Third Voice temptation scene

Purpose: Make belonging seductive.

Template:

1. Player is alone or emotionally exposed.
2. Voice says something useful and true.
3. Voice turns the truth toward the player's want.
4. Player may follow, doubt, or remain silent.

Sample:

> Voice: Cael is afraid. That is why she calls it mercy.  
> Voice: Ister is afraid. That is why he calls it consent.  
> Voice: Wenna is afraid. That is why she calls it answer.  
> Voice: You are afraid too. You call it being between.

### 10.5 Girl surface scene

Purpose: Distinguish her real self from the Voice.

Template:

1. The Voice speaks through or near her.
2. A small bodily detail contradicts it.
3. She says one line that is not useful to anyone.
4. The lens closes.

Sample:

> Voice: She knows where she belongs.  
> [The girl's hand grips the Stone until the nails split.]  
> Girl: My feet are cold.  
> Voice: She is almost through.

## 13. Plot Cutscene Rules for The Third Voice

### 13.1 Opening scene

The opening should establish:

- the protagonist as stranger Intercessor;
- the sealed commission;
- the Threefold March as a place already in crisis;
- the voice as useful before suspicious;
- Aldric as decent and worried;
- the girl as a presence, not a quest object.

Do not over-explain the Anchor, Grid, or future saga.

### 13.2 First visit to the Stone

The Stone scene should be quiet.

Required elements:

- Girl physically present.
- Three claims around her, but no full debate yet.
- Voice says something that feels like sense.
- A small detail that is hers, not the voice's.

Possible line:

> Voice: They are afraid to love her without deciding her.  
> [The girl opens one eye.]  
> Girl: Too loud.  
> Voice: She is nearly ready.

### 13.3 POI resolution scenes

Every POI resolution should end with a procession to the Stone, regardless of shallow or true path.

Do not make resolution feel like quest cleanup.

It should feel like:

> The local crisis has changed shape and is now walking toward the center.

### 13.4 The Convening

The Convening must be written as three sincere mercies colliding.

No one should sound cartoonishly wrong.

Wenna:

> We will not finish her for fear of watching.

Cael:

> We will not leave a child in pain to protect our uncertainty.

Ister:

> We will not cross the threshold she has already marked.

The player/protagonist line must not be a lecture. It may be formal, but it must come from exhaustion, clarity, and refusal.

### 13.5 The girl's line

Her key line should be protected from over-writing.

> It offered me a place too. I said yes.

Do not add explanation directly after it unless the scene absolutely requires it. Let the line breathe.

### 13.6 Ending scene

The ending should not settle the March.

It should show:

- the girl is not killed;
- the dispute has changed, not ended;
- the voice recedes and names the road;
- the protagonist still belongs nowhere;
- choosing non-belonging is the first real choice.

Avoid victory language.

```

For scenes:

```

### 14.3 Mandatory AI checklist

Before accepting generated dialogue, ask:

- Does a common player understand the line without a codex?
- Is there a concrete object or action?
- Does the NPC want something immediate?
- Does the faction appear through custom rather than explanation?
- Is the line too smooth, too poetic, or too complete?
- Does it make the faction both humane and dangerous?
- Could this line be said in any fantasy game? If yes, rewrite.
- Does it include a petty, bodily, or ordinary detail somewhere in the scene?
- Is the weirdness doing a job?
- Does the line leave any remainder?

### 14.4 Forbidden AI habits

Do not generate:

- long abstract monologues;
- faction summaries in dialogue;
- characters saying exactly what their philosophy means;
- villain speeches from the Third Voice;
- random weirdcore nonsense;
- generic medieval fantasy greetings;
- constant solemnity;
- jokes that undercut grief;
- every NPC sounding like a poet;
- every vampire sounding seductive;
- every Church worker sounding sinister;
- every old-world person sounding mystical;
- every Glass-cyborg sounding robotic.

### 14.5 Rewrite procedure for foggy lines

If a line feels like fog, perform this reduction:

1. Identify the abstract claim.
2. Choose an object that carries it.
3. Give the speaker an immediate need involving that object.
4. Make the line shorter.
5. Add the human cost.

Example:

Foggy line:

> Our reverence has become a prison through the amplification of the unanswerable.

Reduced line:

> I want to stand.  
> Then stand.  
> Wenna would hear the chair go empty.

- doors that wait to be opened from inside;
- coats;
- gloves;
- dining tables;
- faint warmth;
- beautiful rooms that remember debt.

Environmental text:

> The door has no lock. It does not need one. The room is waiting for permission to become a room.

## 18. When to Use Documents and Lore Texts

Documents should be short and written as artifacts from the world.

Good document types:

- a cup-washing instruction sheet;
- a Watchfold chair list;
- a Marrowhouse guest card;
- a Church service schedule;
- a letter from Reni to themselves;
- a torn note from Ode;
- a vampire marginal note asking an abyssal question;
- a Glass maintenance tag.

Documents should not explain the setting from above.

### 18.1 Example document - Church cup notice

> PLEASE RETURN COMFORT-CUPS TO THE WARMING CLOTH.  
> Cups found humming after service should be placed on the blue tray.  
> If a mourner asks whether the comfort is real, answer: yes.  
> If they ask whether it is made, send for Sister Linnet.

### 18.2 Example document - Marrowhouse guest card

> This room is yours until you leave or ask us to enter.  
> Refusal does not void hospitality.  
> Blood offered under fear will be refused by the house.  
> Blood offered under gratitude will be tasted twice before acceptance.  
> Gifts do not purchase yes.

### 18.3 Example document - Abyssal marginalia

This should be optional and rare. It should not become the plot.

> The Church says the first wound was loss.  
> The roads say the first error was haste.  
> Our houses say the first sin was taking without consent.  
> But perhaps the first trespass was form.  
> To give a thing an edge is to touch it before it can answer.  
> We ask before we drink.  
> Who asked before the world?

## 19. AI Quality Rubric

Score each generated scene from 1 to 5 on each axis.

### 19.1 Clarity

Can the line be understood immediately?

5: plain, vivid, readable.  
1: foggy, abstract, or lore-dependent.

### 19.2 Embodiment

Does the line involve bodies, objects, spaces, or actions?

5: concrete and situated.  
1: floating idea-speech.

### 19.3 Faction truth

Does the line show the faction's real virtue?

5: the faction's care feels convincing.  
1: faction is a straw man.

### 19.4 Faction danger

Does the line hint at how the care can possess?

5: danger emerges naturally.  
1: danger is absent or cartoonishly evil.

### 19.5 Human need

Does the character want something understandable?

5: immediate want is clear.  
1: character only serves theme.

### 19.6 Remainder

Does the line leave something unresolved?

5: the line lands but does not close the matter.  
1: the line explains itself completely.

Accept lines averaging 4 or higher. Rewrite anything with clarity or embodiment below 3.

## 20. Final Style Summary

The writing style of *The Third Voice* is:

- plain but strange;
- tender but dangerous;
- emotionally direct;
- object-rich;
- custom-driven;
- lightly humorous in ordinary places;
- devastating only when earned;
- never random;
- never foggy;
- never a notecard.

The central writing formula:

> A person needs help.  
> A tradition offers help.  
> The help is real.  
> The help starts to own them.  
> Someone must find a way to keep the care without accepting the possession.

The final test for every line:

> Does this line let a person remain more than the meaning someone wants from them?

If yes, it belongs in Alderamontico.
