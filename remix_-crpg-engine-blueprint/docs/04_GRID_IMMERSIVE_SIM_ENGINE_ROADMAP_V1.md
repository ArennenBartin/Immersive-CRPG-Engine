## TL;DR
- An immersive sim is defined by **systemic consistency, not first-person aesthetics** — Weird West and Caves of Qud prove the genre's principles (global rules applied equally to player and AI, multiple solutions, emergent multiplicative interactions, "systems over scripting") translate fully to a square-grid, turn/AP-based CRPG. Your engine should be architected so that the *same* per-tile simulation and *same* object/event system govern exploration and combat with **no separate "combat mode."**
- The single highest-leverage architectural decision is to make **everything an object composed of data-driven Parts (components) that communicate through a global event bus**, layered over **per-tile property cells** (temperature, liquid, gas, light, sound, occlusion). Caves of Qud — in development since 2007 and released as v1.0 on December 5, 2024 — is the proven reference: it runs the identical seeded, energy-based turn scheduler whether or not you are fighting, and reactions (fire+oil, water+electricity, steam) are resolved deterministically per tick.
- Build in dependency order: **(1) object/Part + event core → (2) tile property layers + AP scheduler → (3) the generic material/reaction system (BotW's 3-rule chemistry) → (4) light/sound perception → (5) verbs → (6) tactical combat layered on the same sim → (7) spatial inventory and "world-reads-your-state" consequence.** Bespoke world-materials like a memory-storing "Glass" crystal are then just special-case Parts on the generic substrate.

## Key Findings

**1. "Immersive sim" is a design philosophy, not a perspective.** The canonical definition emphasizes systems-driven gameplay with a focus on simulation, player agency, and consistent global rules — first-person is a common but non-essential trait. Weird West, released March 31, 2022, is described as "a top-down action role-playing game with elements of the immersive sim genre." It was built by WolfEye Studios — a roughly twenty-person distributed studio co-founded in November 2019 by ex-Arkane president Raphaël Colantonio (18 years at Arkane; co-creator with Harvey Smith of the Dishonored series) and former Arkane executive producer Julien Roby — and delivers full systemic depth (fire propagation across hay and oil, water barrels to douse it, ammo boxes that cook off, dust devils that pick up fire and become flaming cyclones). Its reception was solid rather than spectacular (Metacritic ~76 PC / 73 PS4 / 81 Xbox One), but it stands as your existence proof that grid/top-down loses nothing *essential* to the genre — its weaknesses were repetitive level art, not its systems.

**2. The recurring design principles you must encode globally:**
- **Systemic consistency / "systems over scripting":** rules are few, global, and self-consistent (if water douses fire once, it always does).
- **Rules apply equally to player and AI.** Thief's guards "rarely cheat" — they sense the world through the same light/sound model the player exploits.
- **Multiple solutions / "problems not puzzles":** Ion Storm's Deus Ex design commandments — "There should always be more than one way to get past a game obstacle. Always," and never say "this is where the player does X."
- **Multiplicative, not additive, interactions:** a small verb/element set whose combinations explode in possibility space (Breath of the Wild's explicit design goal).
- **The object-rich world:** Looking Glass's founding belief, stated in the Thief postmortem, that "immersive gameplay emerges from an object-rich world governed by high-quality, self-consistent simulation systems."

**3. Grid-based simulation is a solved problem with a deep reference set.** Caves of Qud simulates per-tile **temperature** (four thresholds: brittle −100, freezing 0, flaming 350, vapor 10,000; relaxes toward ambient 25 each turn), **liquids** (fluidity/evaporativity/viscosity parameters; pooling, flow, mixing, slipperiness, stickiness), **gases** (density-based diffusion with probabilistic spread), **light/occlusion** (objects flagged occluding block both LOS and non-seeping gases), and **electrical/reaction interactions** — all on a square grid, turn-based. Divinity: Original Sin 2 is the key reference for *combat-facing* elemental surfaces (fire+oil=explosion, water+lightning=stun, fire+water=steam that blocks sight, poison+fire=ignite, plus the Elemental Affinity rule where standing on a matching surface discounts spell AP). Dwarf Fortress provides the material-template architecture (generic templates → specific materials inheriting and overriding properties; the wiki notes "there's actually very little difference between, say, leather and iron — they're fundamentally the same thing, just with different properties").

**4. The BotW "chemistry engine" gives you the clean rule model to copy.** As articulated by technical director Takuhiro Dohta at GDC 2017 (with director Hidemaro Fujibayashi and art director Satoru Takizawa), three rules govern the whole sim: (1) elements can change a material's state; (2) elements can change another element's state (water puts out fire); (3) materials cannot change other materials' states. A physics engine handles movement/collision; a separate "chemistry engine" handles elemental state. Crucially, it was prototyped first as a fake-2D game that "only appeared to be 2D. It actually existed as a 3D game space, the extra dimension used to hold data related to chemistry and physics and other computations." This is exactly portable to a tile grid where the cell holds the chemistry data.

## Details

### Area 1 — The immersive-sim contract, encoded as engineering constraints
The "immersive sim contract" is the promise that the world is consistent: any interaction that works once works everywhere, and the game will not arbitrarily forbid a sensible action. Practically this means three hard rules for your codebase:
- **No bespoke scripted exceptions where a systemic rule could apply.** If a torch lights oil, every flame source lights every oil tile.
- **Symmetry of rules.** Whatever perception/damage/reaction model applies to the player applies to NPCs. Thief pioneered this: NPCs sense via the same propagated light and sound the player manipulates, and have a graduated awareness ladder rather than the typical "oblivious/omniscient" binary.
- **Deterministic systems where possible.** The hardcore imsim/CRPG audience values determinism (no to-hit dice on your core systemic interactions); Into the Breach shows that near-perfect information turns tactics into solvable, satisfying puzzles. Save-anywhere (your stated assumption) pairs naturally with determinism: reward experimentation and consequence, not save-scumming-as-RNG-mitigation.

### Area 2 — What grid/top-down imsims actually simulate per tile
Your tile/cell should carry **layered property data** (a "stack" of values per cell), not a single tile type:
- **Material/terrain layer** (floor type, flammable?, conductive?, occluding?, blocks-movement?).
- **Liquid layer** (type, volume/drams, temperature) — Qud's model: fluidity controls flow into adjacent cells (oil ~40 = runny, honey ~10 = sluggish), evaporativity controls disappearance, viscosity creates stickiness (asphalt/honey can stick a creature for up to 12 turns, requiring saves to escape each tile) and slip saves (walking on gel/oil/slime pools forces a roll or you slide uncontrollably).
- **Gas layer** (type, density) — Qud's rule: if a cell's gas density exceeds 10, there's a 25% chance per turn it spreads into 1–4 random adjacent cells, moving 1–30 density; at density ≤10 there's a 50% chance per turn it dissipates entirely; seeping gases pass through walls.
- **Temperature scalar** — relaxes toward ambient each tick by `max(|T−ambient|/50, 5)`.
- **Light level scalar** (for stealth/perception).
- **Sound/disturbance events** (transient, propagate then decay).
Weird West, UnderRail (grid, AP-based, with electronics/fire/chemical ammo and per-NPC stealth tracking), Cogmind, and Tangledeep all confirm grid CRPGs achieve emergent material interaction by making the *cell* the simulation unit, not just the actor.

### Area 3 — Spatial/grid inventory: meaningful friction vs. tedium
Spatial inventory creates decisions when it represents a **real constraint the world cares about**, not busywork:
- **Three constraint models, pick deliberately:** weight (encumbrance → movement/AP penalty, à la UnderRail/Fallout), volume (slot count), and spatial shape (Tetris-packing — Diablo/PoE, Resident Evil 4 attaché, Escape from Tarkov, Deus Ex, System Shock, NEO Scavenger, and Pathologic 2's grid containers, where the Haruspex starts with two 2×4 grids and a 5×5 grid that expand via upgrades). Spatial-shape is the most engaging but risks tedium; the design consensus is that it works when inventory is *not* the focus and limited resources force triage (RE's design), and fails when it rewards obsessive item-box runs.
- **Connect inventory to the sim.** The biggest payoff for an imsim: items are physical objects with grid positions in the world. Dropping, throwing, stacking, and igniting items must use the same object representation whether in a pack or on a floor tile. Container nesting and rotation add depth only if they serve triage decisions.
- **Recommended hybrid for your engine:** slot/shape grid for carried items + a weight budget that converts to AP/movement penalty, so encumbrance feeds the tactical layer (heavy load = fewer combat options), unifying inventory with combat.

### Area 4 — Environmental/material simulation and reaction resolution
Represent each material as a **data-driven property set** (flammable, conductive, fragile, heavy, freezing/flaming/vapor thresholds, fluidity), following Dwarf Fortress's template→specific-material inheritance (a `METAL_TEMPLATE` defines "goes red and melts at high temperature, is heavy"; specific metals reference the template and override). Resolve reactions per tick using the BotW 3-rule model plus DOS2's surface matrix as your content table:
- fire + oil/flammable → spreading fire; fire + water → extinguish + **steam** (steam = vision-blocking gas, a tactical tool); electricity + water → conduct through all connected wet cells (stun); fire + poison/oil gas → explosion; freeze + water → ice (slippery, blocks); acid → degrades armor/objects (note DOS2's exceptions: acid and oil do *not* conduct electricity — exceptions are themselves data).
- **Resolution discipline:** iterate cells with pending changes, apply reactions in a fixed priority order, use seeded RNG for any probabilistic spread so runs are reproducible and debuggable. Qud makes this deterministic via strict tick/segment ordering and a seeded RNG discipline (developers are told never to use global random, only a seeded generator).
- **Weight/pressure plates** fall out naturally once items and creatures have weight and tiles can host trigger Parts.

### Area 5 — Systemic perception, stealth, and AI as first-class grid systems
Thief's model maps cleanly to a grid:
- **Per-tile light level** = the player's visibility input. Thief's "light gem" UI element brightens/dims with how visible you are; the team's founder Paul Neurath has said it was the breakthrough that made the stealth system legible after a year and a half of players getting "confused signals." On a grid, make light a real propagated cell value from light-source Parts (torches, lamps), extinguishable by the sim (water/douse, or Thief's water arrows), so stealth and the material sim are the same system.
- **Noise emission radius:** every action emits a sound event with a loudness; surface material modifies it (carpet/grass quiet, metal loud). Thief propagated sound through a second "audio space" layer of the level — around corners, through open doors — tagged with origin so AI investigate the *source location*, not the player's current position.
- **Graduated alertness state machine:** Thief used three awareness tiers (vague acknowledgement → search mode → combat) with decay back down (guards "give up"), communicated to the player via barks and the gem. Replicate: oblivious → suspicious (investigate last stimulus) → searching → combat, with the player-feedback channel surfaced on the grid via icons/log.
- **Vision model:** Thief used ordered 3D viewcones per AI (XY angle, Z angle, length, acuity, alertness-relevance) with raycasts for LOS. On a grid: viewcone = a set of cells in front of the NPC, weighted by distance and the target cell's light level, gated by an LOS raycast across occluding cells.
- **Turn-based vs real-time:** UnderRail shows the turn-based adaptation — detection checks run per-actor-turn (both on your turn and theirs, with the buffer change halved accordingly) rather than continuously, and the stealth value is tracked per-NPC, not globally.

### Area 6 — Verbs that combine multiplicatively
Design a **small global verb set** that every applicable object exposes: push, pull, throw, drop, stack, climb, burn, douse, freeze, break, hack, wet, electrify, possess/mimic. The multiplicative magic comes from verbs acting on the shared property sim: *throw* a flammable object into a *fire* tile near a *water* barrel near *enemies* on a *metal* floor — and the outcome emerges. Prey's GLOO cannon (immobilize enemies + build climbable platforms + douse fire + bridge gaps + nullify hazards, from one tool) and mimic possession (become any small object → bypass gaps, ambush, or roll through fire as a non-flammable object) are the gold standard of one verb with many systemic uses; Arkane found that letting players copy the Mimic's own power "created a depth of new gameplay options." Tears of the Kingdom's Ultrahand/Fuse extends this further. The rule: **a verb should modify a property, and properties should drive reactions** — never hardcode verb→outcome pairs.

### Area 7 — Deep tactical grid combat as the same simulation
Make combat emergent rather than stat-trading by importing positioning systems proven on grids:
- **Knockback/forced movement as a core verb** (Into the Breach, Tactical Breach Wizards): pushing an enemy into fire, water+electricity, a pit, off a ledge, or into another unit is more interesting than raw damage. TBW makes "throw enemies out windows" the win condition — environmental/window kills bypass all HP and armor, so the design explicitly rewards positioning over damage. Build push/pull/slam as first-class and let the world's hazards do the killing.
- **Perfect-information telegraphing** (Into the Breach): showing enemy intent turns each turn into a solvable spatial puzzle — co-creator Matthew Davis called it "a puzzle game wrapped up in a strategy game"; it pairs perfectly with determinism and save-anywhere.
- **Cover, flanking, overwatch, LOS** (XCOM): directional cover protects only from one axis, flanking removes it and grants +50% crit, overwatch creates reactive zones, and elevation modifies LOS. On a square grid, cover is a property of cell edges; flanking is geometric.
- **Elemental surfaces + verticality + crowd control** (DOS2): the same fire/water/oil/electricity tiles from exploration become combat tools; standing in the right surface can discount ability cost (Elemental Affinity), hazards block movement lanes, and steam clouds blind.
- **Status/condition stacking, height/facing** (Final Fantasy Tactics, Battle Brothers, Troubleshooter, Fell Seal): wet→shocked→stunned chains; height and facing modify hit/damage.
- **Crucially:** all of this uses the *same* fire, liquid, light, and object systems as exploration. A barrel of oil is identical whether you sneak past it or detonate it mid-fight.

### Area 8 — Unifying exploration and combat under one simulation
The architectural answer, proven by Caves of Qud: **there is no separate combat mode.** The same time scheduler (`ActionManager`) runs continuously, advancing time in segments (100 action points) and turns (1000 action points). Every actor accrues "energy" equal to 10× their quickness per turn and spends ~1000 energy per standard action, with unspent energy carrying over; the loop is identical whether walking, exploring, or fighting. (Internally the engine calls action cost "energy"; "action cost" is the public-facing term.) "Combat" is simply actors choosing attack actions inside the same energy/turn loop. Implement: **one scheduler, one tick, one event cascade (`EndAction`/`EndSegment`/`EndTurn`).** Per-tile simulation, status effects, cooldowns, and AI all hang off this single loop. A locked door, a body of water, a light source, and a barrel of oil are the same objects with the same Parts in both contexts; "initiative" is just the moment hostile actors enter the shared scheduler's attention. A deliberate ±10% action-cost jitter varies ordering among equal-speed actors so they don't always act in the same sequence — but it is itself seeded and reproducible.

### Area 9 — Architecture / implementation patterns (the backbone)
Caves of Qud (Freehold Games; programmer Brian Bucklew, designer Jason Grinblat) is the definitive grid-imsim architecture, documented in Bucklew's talk **"Data-Driven Engines of Qud and Sproggiwood"** at the **2015 International Roguelike Developer Conference (IRDC US)** — described on the official press kit as covering "the entity component architectures of Caves of Qud and Sproggiwood" — and a follow-up IRDC 2016 talk on AI. The pattern was influenced by Scott Bilas's GDC 2002 talk "A Data-Driven Game Object System":
- **Everything is a GameObject composed of Parts (components).** Parts are declared as data in `ObjectBlueprints.xml`. Objects inherit down a tree from a single generic root `Object` (e.g., Knollworm Skull → BaseHelmet → Armor → Item → InorganicObject → PhysicalObject → Object), so shared behavior lives in base blueprints and specialization is pure Part composition. You make a flaming gauntlet by adding a `TemperatureOnHit` Part to a generic Armor object — no new code. The wiki notes you "might combine a `LiquidVolume` part with a `MeleeWeapon` part to create a weapon that holds liquid."
- **A global event bus is the universal communication mechanism.** Per the modding wiki: "Events are the basic building block that most of Caves of Qud's mechanics are built upon. When any object spawns, attacks, moves, dies and etcetera: an event is fired," giving any registered listener a chance to react. You `FireEvent` on an object; it dispatches `HandleEvent` to all the object's Parts; returning false halts propagation (cooperative veto). The modern system uses **pooled, strongly-typed events** (`ModPooledEvent<T>`, `Event.FromPool()`) to avoid allocation, with `WantEvent(ID, cascade)` for fast filtering — and the wiki cautions this "will be executed every time an event is fired so keep it lean."
- **Events cascade into contained objects via a bitfield** (`CASCADE_EQUIPMENT` / `INVENTORY` / `SLOTS` / `COMPONENTS`) so an event fired on a creature reaches its equipped weapon and worn armor. Combat damage (e.g., a `TemperatureOnHit` Part reading the "Defender" parameter off an "AttackerHit" event), heat-on-hit, and even conversations all ride the same bus.
- **`IActivePart` / `IPoweredPart`** give data-configured Parts rich conditional behavior (charge use, breakage, EMP/power states) with a standard "is this operational?" gate — no bespoke scripting.
- **Determinism:** seeded RNG discipline (`GetSeededRandomGenerator()`, never global random) + strict segment/turn ordering.
- **Layering generic → specific:** define generic material templates and generic reaction rules first; bespoke world-materials (your memory-crystal "Glass") become a specialized blueprint that adds unique Parts (e.g., a `MemoryStore` Part, a custom reaction listener) on top of the generic substrate. This is exactly how Qud adds exotic liquids and randomizing "warm static" as data on the same liquid system, and how Dwarf Fortress adds a custom "star metal" as a template-derived material restricted to a specific civilization.

For an ECS implementation: model material reactions as **reactive systems** that run only on cells/entities whose relevant components changed this tick (the EnTT/EcsRx "reactive system" pattern — "instead of updating all 100 entities… only update the 10 changed"), and resolve via an **event/effect pipeline** in a fixed order for determinism. Treat events as function calls or as components attached to affected entities, keeping logic in systems.

### Area 10 — The "systematically and spiritually checked" feeling
The sense of a world that *tests, judges, and processes* the player emerges from systemic pressure plus consequence, not scripted gates:
- **Reputation/territory systems that read your state and gate passage** — Pathologic 2 tracks per-district reputation (Welcome → Unwelcome → Hated); cross a threshold and stores deny you, then at Hated the local Militia "openly attack them in the street." Some districts (the Station, Warehouses, Factory, Cape, the Steppe) have no reputation status and "cannot judge the player character for crimes committed," so geography itself becomes a moral checkpoint — you can lure NPCs onto neutral ground to act with impunity. This is a literal "border-crossing" model you can build per-region on a grid.
- **Survival attrition that the world imposes indifferently** — NEO Scavenger's per-turn hunger/thirst/fatigue/temperature (driven by real autumn-Michigan weather data) and infection make the environment an ever-present examiner; players note "the real cool stuff… is a result of game mechanics interacting with each other," writing a different story each run.
- **Skill/perception checks that surface what the world "sees" in you** — Disco Elysium contains **10,287 passive skill checks** (plus 211 anti-passives), ranging from 115 Hand/Eye Coordination checks to 899 Empathy checks; a passive passes automatically without a roll if `skill + modifiers + 6 ≥ difficulty`, constantly reading your character's internal state to reveal or withhold content. On a grid CRPG, a parallel system can have the world continuously evaluate the player's stats, gear, reputation, and visible state to gate dialogue, passage, prices, and reactions — the feeling of being *processed*.
- **Irreversible world-state** (your stated interest): persistent consequences — a town you slaughtered stays a ghost town (Weird West's state "is persistent across the entire campaign") — make the world feel like it remembers and judges, even under save-anywhere.

## Recommendations

**Build in this dependency order. Each stage is independently testable and unlocks the next.**

**Stage 1 — The object/Part + event core (highest leverage; everything depends on it).**
- Implement GameObjects as ID + a bag of data-driven Parts; load from a blueprint file with an inheritance tree from one root Object.
- Implement a global event bus: `FireEvent` → dispatch `HandleEvent` to all Parts, with veto-by-returning-false and a cascade bitfield into contained objects. Use a pooled, strongly-typed event design from the start to avoid allocation churn.
- *Benchmark to proceed:* you can create a new interactive object (e.g., a flammable crate that breaks when hit) entirely in data, with no new code.

**Stage 2 — Tile property layers + the AP/energy scheduler.**
- Give each cell layered properties (material, liquid, gas, temperature, light, sound).
- Build ONE continuous scheduler (energy = k× speed per turn; actions cost energy; carry-over; seeded ±jitter optional) with `EndAction/EndSegment/EndTurn` events. No combat mode.
- *Benchmark:* time passes and per-tile temperature relaxes toward ambient identically whether or not enemies are present.

**Stage 3 — The generic material/reaction system (the emergent-depth multiplier).**
- Encode the BotW 3 rules + a DOS2-style reaction table over material/liquid/gas/temperature properties. Resolve deterministically with seeded RNG and fixed priority order.
- *Benchmark:* fire+oil spreads, water+fire makes steam, electricity+water stuns — and these behave identically in exploration and combat. **This stage, combined with Stages 1–2, produces the most emergent depth for the least implementation cost — prioritize it above everything that follows.**
- *Current implementation:* MVP-complete in `src/engine-core/immersiveSim.ts` for the current architecture: `IMMERSIVE_REACTION_RULES` exposes the fixed-priority table; water+fire creates steam/doused residue and consumes source fire, fire+ice creates meltwater/steam and consumes source ice/fire, oil+fire creates fire/smoke/burned state, electricity+water creates conductive electricity/stun, poison+fire creates toxic gas/smoke/contamination/poison, cold+water creates slippery ice/frozen/slow and consumes source water, and acid+material creates fumes/corrosion/damaged/weaken. Propagation now spreads fire into adjacent flammables, chains electricity through adjacent wet cells, and diffuses smoke/poison gas into neighboring open cells. Reactions commit save-backed fields, surface layers, cell conditions, tile-layer state, world facts, actor statuses, and save-backed multi-axis actor physical states (`temperature`, `wetness`, `heat`, `chill`, `charge`, `coating`, `toxicity`, labels). Later depth can move the fixed table into package-authored data, but the Stage 3 benchmark is met.

**Stage 4 — Perception: per-tile light + propagated sound + alertness AI.**
- Light as propagated cell value from source Parts (extinguishable by the sim); sound events with material-modified loudness propagating through the cell graph; graduated NPC alertness (oblivious→suspicious→searching→combat) with investigation of the source cell and decay; per-actor-turn detection checks. Surface AI state to the player (Thief's light-gem lesson).
- *Benchmark:* a guard investigates a noise's origin cell and gives up if it finds nothing; dousing a torch creates a usable shadow.
- *Current implementation:* MVP-complete in `src/engine-core/immersiveSim.ts` for the current architecture. `createImmersivePerceptionSnapshotFromV1` derives light, sound, fire, smoke, danger-gas, and visible-player stimuli from Stage 2 tile layers, then scores NPCs through radius, tile LOS, viewcone, and facing gates. `advanceImmersivePerceptionForSave` can run after scheduler segments, writes `alertness`, `alert_score`, `last_stimulus`, and `investigation_target_cell` into entity state, queues investigation/report tasks, emits `immersive_perception_alert` facts, decays stale alerts until actors give up, emits `immersive_perception_gave_up` facts, and writes `flags.immersive_stealth_feedback`. Play Mode now advances this outside combat and renders a stealth gem, seeing/alerted counts, top alert rows, and per-NPC canvas alert badges. Studio shows Stage 4 stimuli and alerts. Later depth can add richer acuity/authored senses, barks/audio, and authored stealth content, but the Stage 4 benchmark is met.

**Stage 5 — The global verb set.**
- push/pull/throw/drop/stack/climb/burn/douse/freeze/break/hack/electrify, each modifying properties (never hardcoded to outcomes). Add one signature systemic tool in the GLOO/mimic spirit.
- *Benchmark:* a player solves an unplanned obstacle by combining ≥2 verbs the designers didn't script.
- *Current implementation:* MVP-complete at headless scope in `src/engine-core/immersiveSim.ts`, with the newer numeric chemistry runtime now owning Play Mode elemental resolution. `IMMERSIVE_GLOBAL_VERBS` exposes the canonical verb registry, and `applyImmersiveGlobalVerbToSave` covers `push`, `pull`, `throw`, `drop`, `stack`, `climb`, `burn`, `douse`, `freeze`, `break`, `hack`, `wet`, `electrify`, `foam`, and `mimic` as the headless/fallback global-verb surface. Movement verbs write save-backed object placement/condition deltas and impact sound; `drop` moves inventory into world item placement state; `stack` and `climb` create traversal-support properties; `hack` changes access state at headless scope but is not currently player-facing; `break` removes object placements; `mimic` records actor form state. In Play Mode, elemental verbs (`burn`, `douse`, `freeze`, `wet`, `electrify`, `foam`) resolve first through `applyChemistryVerbToSave`, which pushes numeric axes, stores `PlaySave.chemistry`, advances settle ticks, projects derived world deltas, and allows fire/ice/electricity/foam state to keep evolving. Play Mode surfaces Phase 2 elemental verbs through world deltas, actor badges, and the player Body HUD, and surfaces Phase 3 non-hack movement/traversal verbs through highlighted object/cell targeting. Studio shows the verb registry and recent verb facts. Later work should broaden authored content and expose form/identity verbs plus richer context gates as live Play Mode affordances.

**Stage 6 — Tactical combat layer on the same sim.**
- Knockback/forced-movement as core verbs; directional cover on cell edges; flanking; overwatch reactive zones; status stacking; optional perfect-information telegraphing; height/facing modifiers.
- *Benchmark:* an environmental/positioning kill (push into hazard) is more efficient than raw damage in common cases.
- *Current implementation:* MVP-complete at headless scope and Play MVP scope in `src/engine-core/immersiveSim.ts`. `createImmersiveCombatTacticalSnapshotFromV1` derives actors, teams, facing, height, statuses, directional cover edges, overwatch zones, and telegraphed hostile intents from the shared map/save/tile-layer state. `applyImmersiveCombatForcedMovementToSave` spends scheduler action energy, pushes a target actor along valid grid cells, runs Stage 3 reactions at the destination, applies reaction statuses and tile hazard damage, triggers opposing overwatch zones, updates actor HP/death state, and emits durable forced-movement/overwatch facts. `applyImmersiveCombatAttackToSave` spends scheduler energy and resolves attacks with LOS, directional cover mitigation, flanking bypass/bonus, height modifiers, facing modifiers, HP/death writes, and `immersive_combat_attack_resolved` facts. Player-set overwatch is now live: `applyImmersivePlayerOverwatchToSave` arms a save-backed reactive zone (`flags.immersive_overwatch_player`, an `immersive_combat_overwatch_set` fact, and the tactical snapshot's zone cells), and `applyImmersiveOverwatchToMovementSave` resolves the same trigger rule against ordinary enemy movement that forced movement already obeyed. Studio shows tactical actors, cover edges, overwatch zones, and telegraphed intents. Play Mode surfaces a Stage 6 **Shove** action and an **Overwatch** action on the combat tactics bar (armed state, reaction popups/logs, XP on overwatch kills), routes in-combat melee through the Stage 6 attack resolver, previews faced-target base damage/estimated hit/cover/flank/height modifiers, echoes those modifiers through popups/logs, and paints overwatch zones, hostile intent target cells, and actor-following hostile intent tethers on the canvas. Cover edges remain in the tactical snapshot/HUD math and Studio inspection, but are no longer painted as a map-wide canvas overlay. Later work should author more encounter content and tune numbers, but the Stage 6 benchmark is met.

**Stage 7 — Spatial inventory + "world-reads-your-state" consequence.**
- Shape/slot grid + weight→AP penalty; items as world objects. Per-region reputation gates; survival attrition; passive-check-style world evaluation; persistent irreversible world-state.
- *Benchmark:* the world denies/permits passage based on the player's tracked state without a scripted trigger.
- *Current implementation:* MVP-complete at headless scope and Play MVP scope in `src/schema/game.ts`, `src/engine-core/immersiveSim.ts`, and `PlayMode.tsx`. Items can author `spatial` profiles with shape, weight, bulk, stack-limit hints, and survival-restoration consumable effects; maps can author `regions` with faction thresholds, survival deltas, passive checks, and irreversible denial flags. `createImmersiveSpatialInventorySnapshotFromSave` packs inventory stacks into a deterministic slot grid using authored shapes when present, derives fallback shapes from bulk, computes total weight, overflow, action-energy penalty, and projects inventory stacks as actor-held world-object refs. `evaluateImmersiveWorldStateForSave` reads the player's current map region, authored region rules, reputation, survival flags, inventory load, and passive checks to produce permit/deny/warning gates without a scripted trigger. `advanceImmersiveWorldStateForSave` applies authored and caller-provided survival attrition deltas, persists last region/permitted/denied flags, stores the current inventory AP penalty, writes irreversible consequence flags for region denial/survival crisis/passive denial, and emits `immersive_world_state_evaluated` facts. Play Mode surfaces condition/load pressure, region denial/warning feedback, denial gates for movement/non-drop global verbs, survival-restoring field ration consumption, and S6 workstation production/collection for tonics and field rations; Studio shows spatial inventory load, projected inventory objects, region gates, consequence flags, and recent world-state facts. Later work can add broader authored consequences/content, but the Stage 7 benchmark is met.

**Adding bespoke world-materials (e.g., "Glass"):** only after Stages 1–3 are solid. Implement it as a specialized blueprint adding unique Parts (`MemoryStore`, custom reaction listeners) on the generic material substrate — a special case, never a parallel system.

**Thresholds that change the plan:** If reaction resolution becomes nondeterministic or un-debuggable, stop and fix tick-ordering/seeding before adding content (Qud's discipline). If spatial-inventory playtesting shows players doing item-box busywork rather than triage, cut to a simpler weight/slot model. If AI perception feels unfair, add more player-feedback channels *before* tuning the model itself (Thief's lesson: the system felt broken until the light gem made state legible).

## Caveats
- **Sourcing quality:** The deepest architectural specifics (Qud's event pooling, cascade bitfield, energy scheduler, temperature/gas rules) are documented primarily on the official Caves of Qud modding wiki, which quotes engine source, plus Bucklew's recorded IRDC talks; a verbatim transcript of the 2015 talk was not obtainable, and one community scheduler write-up (qudzoo) is unofficial though high-quality. Treat exact internal method names as indicative rather than canonical.
- **Determinism vs. emergence tension:** Maximum emergence (many interacting probabilistic systems) fights debuggability. The proven resolution is seeded RNG + fixed resolution order; budget engineering time for this from the start, not as a retrofit.
- **Spatial inventory is double-edged:** every cited source warns it tips into tedium if it becomes the focus. It is a lower priority than the material/perception/verb systems for *immersive-sim feel*.
- **Forward-looking claims:** WolfEye's next first-person immersive sim and UnderRail 2: Infusion (which explicitly adds "immersive sim elements" and interactive environmental objects to a grid RPG) are in development as of this writing; their designs are announced/described, not shipped, so treat them as directional intent rather than proven implementations.
- **Scope realism:** Caves of Qud was in development for over fifteen years (since 2007, first beta 2010, v1.0 December 5, 2024) and Dwarf Fortress far longer; deep material simulation is a long-horizon investment. The staged roadmap above is designed so that Stages 1–3 alone yield a recognizably immersive-sim experience before the later stages compound it.
